üìò Lesson 1: What is Phishing?

Phishing is when scammers try to trick you into giving them your personal information. They might pretend to be someone you trust, like your bank, a government agency, or a company you use.

EXAMPLE

Fake email scenario: You receive an email that looks like it's from your bank saying "Your account has been locked! Click here to verify your details immediately."

This is likely a phishing attempt. Your bank will never ask you to click a link in an email to verify your account.

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Point: Phishing messages often create a sense of urgency or fear to make you act without thinking.

Quick Check

üìò Lesson 2: Common Scam Tactics

Scammers use different tricks to catch you off guard. Here are the most common ones to watch out for:

  • 1
    The "Urgent" Scam

    Messages claiming your account will be closed or you'll be fined if you don't act NOW.

  • 2
    The "Prize" Scam

    "Congratulations! You've won a prize!" But you need to pay a fee or share bank details to claim it.

  • 3
    The "Tech Support" Scam

    Pop-ups or calls claiming your computer has a virus and you need to pay for help.

  • 4
    The "Grandparent" Scam

    Someone calls pretending to be a family member in trouble who needs money immediately.

    • ‚úÖ Remember: Legitimate companies won't pressure you to act immediately or ask for payments in unusual ways (like gift cards).

    Quick Check

    üìò Lesson 3: Anatomy of a Scam Email

    Here are 5 red flags to look for in emails:

    • COMPARE THESE

    Fake: From: "support@amaz0n-security.com" - "Your Amazon account suspended! Click here to verify immediately"

    Real: From: "amazon.co.uk" - "Your recent order summary" (no urgent demands, just order info)

    üìò Lesson 4: Checking Links Safely

    Before clicking any link, here's how to check if it's safe:

  • 1
    Hover, don't click

    On a computer, hover your mouse over the link. Look at the bottom of your browser - the real web address will appear.

  • 2
    Check for HTTPS

    Secure websites start with https:// (the 's' stands for secure). Look for the padlock icon üîí.

  • 3
    Type it yourself

    If unsure, open your browser and type the company's web address yourself. Don't use the link from the email.

    • ‚ö†Ô∏è Never click links in unexpected messages! Even if they look real, go directly to the website by typing the address yourself.

    üìò Lesson 1: Creating Strong Passwords

    A strong password is your first line of defense. Follow these guidelines:

    • Use at least 12 characters
    • Mix uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols
    • Avoid common words or personal info
    • Never reuse passwords across different sites
    EXAMPLE

    Weak: "password123" or "John1950"

    Strong: "C0mp13x!P@ssw0rd" or use a passphrase like "Purple-Panda-Flying-99!"

    üìò Lesson 2: Using Password Managers

    A password manager securely stores all your passwords. You only need to remember one master password. Benefits include:

  • ‚úì Creates strong, random passwords for you
  • ‚úì Auto-fills login details on websites
  • ‚úì Alerts you if a site is fake

    • Popular options: LastPass, 1Password, or Bitwarden (free).

    üìò Lesson 3: Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

    2FA adds a second layer of security. Even if someone steals your password, they can't log in without the second step.

    Enable 2FA wherever possible! Methods include:

    • Text message code
    • Authentication app (Google Authenticator)
    • Physical security key

    üìò Lesson 4: Account Recovery Options

    Set up recovery options in case you get locked out:

    • Add a recovery email address
    • Add a phone number for SMS recovery
    • Keep backup codes in a safe place

    üìò Lesson 1: Secure Websites (HTTPS)

    Always look for "https://" at the beginning of a web address. The 's' stands for secure and means your connection is encrypted.

    CHECK FOR THE PADLOCK üîí

    In your browser's address bar, a padlock icon means the site uses HTTPS. Never enter personal information on a site without this.

    üìò Lesson 2: Public Wi-Fi Risks

    Public Wi-Fi (in cafes, airports) is not secure. Others on the same network could potentially see your activity.

    Avoid banking or shopping on public Wi-Fi. If you must, use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) or your phone's mobile hotspot.

    üìò Lesson 3: Browser Security Settings

    Modern browsers have built-in security features. Ensure they are enabled:

    • Pop-up blocker (should be ON)
    • "Safe Browsing" (warns you about dangerous sites)
    • Automatic updates (keep your browser updated)

    üìò Lesson 4: Privacy Protection

    Reduce what websites can track:

    • Clear your browsing history and cookies regularly
    • Use private or "incognito" mode for sensitive searches
    • Consider using a search engine like DuckDuckGo that doesn't track you

    üìò Lesson 1: Privacy Settings Overview

    Each social platform has privacy settings. Key settings to check:

    • Who can see your posts (set to "Friends" not "Public")
    • Who can send you friend requests
    • Whether your profile appears in search engines

    üìò Lesson 2: Friend Request Scams

    Scammers create fake profiles. Be cautious if:

    You receive a request from a stranger with very few friends. Or a duplicate request from someone you're already friends with.

    üìò Lesson 3: What to Share/Not Share

    Avoid posting:

    • Your full birth date
    • Home address
    • When you're going on holiday (burglars watch for this)
    • Photos of tickets (barcodes can be copied)

    üìò Lesson 4: Blocking and Reporting

    Know how to block and report users. If someone harasses you or seems fake, use the "Report" function. Blocking prevents them from contacting you.

    üìò Lesson 5: Location Sharing Risks

    Turn off location services for social media apps when not needed. Posting photos from home can reveal your exact location.

    üìò Lesson 1: Secure Payment Methods

    Use credit cards for online shopping‚Äîthey offer better fraud protection than debit cards. PayPal also adds a layer of security.

    üìò Lesson 2: Spotting Fake Online Stores

    Warning signs: prices too good to be true, no contact info, poor English, no reviews elsewhere. Check trustpilot.com for reviews.

    üìò Lesson 3: Banking App Safety

    Only download your bank's official app from the Apple App Store or Google Play Store. Enable biometric login (fingerprint/face).

    üìò Lesson 4: Credit vs Debit Cards

    Credit cards use the bank's money, so if fraud occurs, your own cash isn't frozen. Debit cards draw directly from your account.

    üìò Lesson 5: Refund and Dispute Rights

    In the UK, Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act protects purchases over ¬£100 made on a credit card. Your bank also has a "chargeback" scheme for debit cards.

    Keep your devices safe

    üìò Lesson 1: Software Updates

    Updates fix security holes. Enable automatic updates on your computer, phone, and apps.

    Ignoring updates leaves you vulnerable to known hacks.

    üìò Lesson 2: Antivirus Protection

    Use reputable antivirus software. Windows comes with "Windows Security" (formerly Defender) which is good. Keep it active.

    üìò Lesson 3: Backing Up Your Data

    If your device is hacked or fails, backups save your photos and files. Follow the "3-2-1 rule": 3 copies, on 2 different media, with 1 off-site (e.g., cloud).

    üìò Lesson 4: Secure Device Disposal

    Before selling or recycling a device, factory reset it. For computers, use a program to wipe the drive so data can't be recovered.
